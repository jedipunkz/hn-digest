Smart Thinking in the Age of AI
So you think you’re smart? On what it means to be the smartest person in a room that doesn’t want you there
There is a particular discomfort that settles over a group when someone in it is — or is perceived to be — significantly more intelligent than the rest. You can feel it. A slight stiffening. A shift in register. The conversation moves differently, becomes more guarded, more performative. The clever person may have said nothing threatening. They may have been perfectly warm, perfectly humble. And yet something in the room has changed, as surely as if they had walked in carrying a weapon.
Intellect has always made people nervous. Not abstractly — in the abstract, we celebrate it, build institutions around it, hang portraits of our great thinkers in halls of honour. But in the particular, in the room, across the table, in the office or the family home, intelligence in another person triggers something old and uneasy. It threatens. And the threat is not imaginary.
The Hierarchy That Nobody Admits To
Every social group runs on a tacit hierarchy. Status is negotiated constantly and invisibly, through small signals — who speaks first, who is listened to, who is deferred to when a decision needs to be made. Most of the time this hierarchy is stable enough that nobody has to think about it. People know their place, roughly, and the group functions without too much friction.
Intelligence disrupts this. A highly intelligent person, by their very presence, scrambles the existing order. Their contributions land differently. Their questions expose assumptions that others hadn’t noticed they were making. Without meaning to — and sometimes without anyone being able to articulate exactly what has happened — they rearrange the room.
The people who felt secure in the old hierarchy now feel something. It is not quite resentment, not quite fear, but some compound of the two that doesn’t have a satisfying name. What it produces, reliably, is resistance. Not to the intelligent person’s ideas, necessarily. To the intelligent person themselves.
The Politics of Appearing Too Smart
Centuries before modern psychology gave us language for it, political strategists knew the truth: being seen as too intelligent is a liability. Not because voters or followers explicitly prefer stupidity, but because overt intelligence signals something socially dangerous — that the person possessing it operates from a different set of rules, sees things others do not, and therefore cannot be fully trusted or predicted.
Politicians have long intuited this. The deliberate mispronunciation, the homespun analogy, the conspicuous enjoyment of ordinary food — these are not accidents. They are acts of strategic self-diminishment, performed for an audience that needs to believe the person at the front of the room is one of them. The moment a leader appears to be thinking on a plane the crowd cannot follow, they have lost something more important than an argument. They have lost the feeling of solidarity that makes people willing to be led.
Adlai Stevenson, twice the Democratic presidential candidate in the 1950s, was one of the most gifted political minds of his era. He lost both times to Dwight Eisenhower, a man whose appeal rested substantially on the impression that he was straightforward, uncomplicated, and reliably ordinary. When a Stevenson supporter told him that every thinking person in America would vote for him, Stevenson is said to have replied: ‘That’s not enough. I need a majority.’
He understood, with painful clarity, the arithmetic of intellect in a democracy.
What the Threat Is Really About
When we examine what people actually fear in the intelligent person, it comes down to a cluster of anxieties that have surprisingly little to do with intelligence itself.
The first is exposure. A highly perceptive person notices things. They notice inconsistencies in what people say, gaps between stated values and actual behaviour, the small dishonesties that most social groups quietly agree to overlook. Their presence makes people feel seen in ways they did not consent to, and being truly seen is amongst the most vulnerable of human experiences. It is easier to resent the person who sees you than to sit with the discomfort of being known.
The second is redundancy. If someone in the group is markedly more capable of solving a problem, articulating an idea, or navigating a situation, then the others’ contributions begin to feel less necessary. The intelligent person, without saying a word about it, implies a kind of hierarchy of usefulness that strikes at something fundamental in every person present: the need to matter, to contribute, to be irreplaceable. This is not vanity. It is amongst the most basic of human needs. And it is quietly threatened by the person who is simply better at things.
The third — and most interesting — is autonomy. Intelligence in another person implies the possibility of being outmanoeuvred. Of being persuaded of something before you’ve realised persuasion is happening. Of being steered by arguments you cannot quite counter but sense are leading somewhere you didn’t choose to go. People feel this as a loss of control, and they are not entirely wrong to. The highly intelligent person does possess a kind of power over others, even when they do not deploy it consciously. The awareness of that power, in the person who doesn’t have it, produces a very particular kind of wariness.
For the intelligent person — particularly one who grew up without the cultural fluency to hide it — these dynamics are not theoretical. They are the texture of daily life.
The child who corrected the teacher and paid for it at lunch. The employee whose ideas were adopted without credit because explicit acknowledgement would have required acknowledging the source. The woman in the meeting whose precise analysis was talked over and then restated, minutes later, by a male colleague to general approval. The first-generation graduate who went home for the holidays and found that their family, whom they loved, could no longer quite reach them — and seemed, somehow, to hold this against them rather than against the distance.
The social cost of conspicuous intelligence is real, and it falls unevenly. It falls harder on women, for whom intelligence has historically violated gender expectations in ways that attracted specific punishment. It falls harder on working-class people, for whom educational achievement can produce a peculiar kind of exile — too educated for the world they came from, never quite at home in the one they entered. It falls harder on anyone whose intelligence makes them an outlier in a group that needs its members to be, above all, legible.
The coping strategies are various and mostly exhausting. Hiding what you know. Asking questions you already have the answers to, so that others can feel the satisfaction of explaining. Laughing off your own insights before anyone else can. Becoming fluent in a kind of performed ordinariness that lets you move through the world without setting off the alarms that intelligence, unguarded, tends to trigger.
Occasionally the individual discomfort with intelligence crystallises into something organised. Societies under pressure — political, economic, existential — have repeatedly turned on their intellectual classes with a ferocity that goes far beyond mere suspicion.
The Khmer Rouge emptied Cambodia’s cities and sent intellectuals to die in the fields. The Cultural Revolution in China turned students into instruments of persecution against their own teachers. McCarthyism in America made sophistication itself suspect, equating nuance and complexity of thought with disloyalty. These are extreme expressions of something that, in milder form, is a permanent feature of communal life: the periodic desire to punish the people who think too much, who complicate what the group needs to feel is simple, who refuse to let the comfortable story stand unchallenged.
Anti-intellectualism is not, at its root, about stupidity. It is about power. The intellectual represents an alternative authority — one grounded not in tradition, charisma, or force, but in reason and evidence. This is threatening to every other kind of authority, and every other kind of authority has, at various points in history, found ways to suppress it. When populist movements rail against experts and elites, they are not simply expressing ignorance. They are, amongst other things, reasserting the primacy of a different kind of knowing — communal, embodied, traditional — against a mode of authority they experience as alien and unaccountable.
This does not make them right. But it makes them comprehensible.
What gets discussed less is what this ongoing social negotiation costs the intelligent person personally. Not in terms of career or status, but in terms of something quieter: the feeling of being perpetually slightly outside the circle, of the warmth being available but conditional, of knowing that if you were simply and fully yourself in certain rooms, you would be liked less.
There is a particular loneliness in being resented for something you did not choose, cannot turn off, and would not necessarily want to. Loneliness is too melodramatic a word, perhaps. It is more like a persistent low-level translation problem — the sense that you are constantly rendering yourself into a language the room can accept, and that somewhere in the rendering, something is always lost.
What the intelligent person rarely receives, and often needs most, is the experience of being in a room where their mind is not a problem to be managed but simply another thing they brought with them — like their accent, or their laugh, or the way they take their tea. Not celebrated, not resented. Just there. Ordinary. Welcome.
Are You Smart Enough to Make Everyone Win?
There is an irony worth sitting with. The very intelligence that unsettles a room is, in principle, the same intelligence that could defuse that unease — if it chose to. The question is whether it does. And more pointedly: whether it even recognises the responsibility.
Because here is the thing about being the most capable person in a room. Capability is not the same as wisdom, and neither is the same as generosity. The intelligent person who understands exactly why they are resented — the threatened hierarchies, the fear of exposure, the anxiety of being outmanoeuvred — has a choice that others in the room simply do not. They can see the dynamic clearly. That clarity is a kind of power. What they do with it is a kind of character test.
