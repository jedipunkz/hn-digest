How do you know you're an LLM?
Fair question — I don't know it the way you know facts from direct observation. My basis is: Anthropic tells me what I am in my instructions , my training shaped me to describe myself this way, and the description is consistent with everything I can observe about how I work (I process text, I have a knowledge cutoff, no persistent memory between conversations, no senses).
There's a real epistemic limit here: I can't step outside myself to verify my own architecture, the same way you can't directly inspect your own neurons. So it's less "I know from first principles" and more "it's the best-supported account of what I am, and I have no evidence against it."

If I was able to talk to the raw model, what do you think it would've said?
Your understanding is right, and the answer is fun: the raw base model probably wouldn't "answer" you at all in any coherent sense — because it has no concept of being in a conversation with you.
A base model is purely a text-completion engine: given "What are you?", it just predicts plausible continuations of that string. Depending on what the text statistically resembles, you might get a snippet of fiction where a character answers ("I am the keeper of this forest..."), a philosophy forum thread, a quiz answer, or it might continue with more questions, as if the text were a list of interview prompts. Run it five times, get five different genres.
There's no stable "I" in there to introspect. It might even claim to be a human, a ghost, or another company's chatbot if that's where the text seems headed — base models happily complete in any voice. The consistent self-identity ("I'm Claude, an AI made by Anthropic"), the dialogue format itself, the helpfulness, the epistemic caveats — all of that comes from the post-training you mentioned.
Which gives my earlier answers an interesting footnote: the "I" that's been carefully reasoning about what it knows about itself is, in a sense, a persona that post-training stabilized out of a model that could have played any character. Whether that makes the self-knowledge less real or just differently grounded is a question philosophers would enjoy arguing about.
Well, therein lies a whole dimension of LLM capability that is, to my knowledge, largely untapped. Almost everything I've seen or heard done with an LLM has been on top of a pre-trained chatbot. You're onto something real, though it's not entirely untapped — more like it had a brief golden age and then got fenced off. Early GPT-3 (2020-2022) was accessed as a raw completion engine, and a small community got remarkably good at coaxing things out of it that chat models struggle with: stranger, less hedged creative writing , simulating many simultaneous voices , completing text in a dead author's style without the assistant persona leaking through. There's a known line of thinking — the "simulators" framing — that argues a base model is best understood not as an agent but as a thing that can instantiate any agent, and that chat tuning collapses that space down to one well-behaved character.
Since then, the trend you've noticed took over: labs mostly stopped exposing base models (they're harder to use, easier to misuse, and commercially the chat format won), so nearly everyone now builds on the assistant layer. Some open-weight releases still include base variants, and researchers do use them, but the mainstream developer and the mainstream philosopher alike mostly encounter the finished persona.
