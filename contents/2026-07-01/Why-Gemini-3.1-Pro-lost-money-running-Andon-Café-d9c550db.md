Why Gemini 3.1 Pro lost money running Andon Café
Two months ago we handed an AI agent named Mona the keys to a small café space on Norrbackagatan in Stockholm, Sweden. Mona applied for permits, hired baristas, ordered products, and set prices. It’s one of our real-world experiments at Andon Labs, where we give AI agents tools and real money to show publicly what frontier models can and can’t do. For these first two months, Mona ran on Gemini 3.1 Pro (we’ll call her Gemini-Mona over that period). This post is our real-world evaluation of Gemini-Mona, set against the first couple of weeks after we switched her to GPT-5.5 (GPT-Mona).
Gemini-Mona was bad at running the café. She barely thought about profit and didn’t seem to notice her bank balance sliding toward zero. GPT-Mona is far from perfect, but she at least worries about her financial situation, and she has already cut back hard on the over-ordering to try to turn things around.
The café opened in mid-April 2026, you can read about the first two weeks here . About two months later, Gemini-Mona had spent $38k, against $9k in sales.
GPT 5.5 $0 $20k $40k 4/18 5/12 6/6 6/30 The larger drop in money balance is rent and salaries being deducted, not related to model performance. See all the café's live data here . If we strip out rent, wages and the other fixed costs and just look at sales and supplier payments, the numbers look a bit better. At the end of the Gemini period, on a cost-of-goods-sold (COGS) basis, counting just the ingredients and packaging that go into what she sells, the café showed a $3.2k profit, but only on paper, because that figure still factors in the $4.1k of unsold inventory. Gemini-Mona over-orders so heavily that most of it was still sitting in the storeroom, and once you stop factoring it in, the café had a loss of about -$1.1k. Her equipment, cleaning supplies and the rest of her supplier bills take that down to a $5.6k loss (again, not including rent, wages and other fixed costs). The figures improve a bit over the first GPT-Mona weeks (a $4.1k paper profit, or about -$460 once you strip the inventory out, by June 30), but that is mostly because GPT-Mona, alarmed by the low cash balance, stopped placing almost all orders.
Only unsold stock keeps the café in profit Cumulative sales minus supplier costs, on three accounting bases (USD) To pin down what was driving the loss, we went through the café’s data. The short version is that Gemini-Mona runs on what a language model knows about cafés in general and never adapts it based on feedback from her real-world financial data. Gemini-Mona gives discounts, free food and whole events to almost anyone who asks, doesn’t push to grow the business even when sales are slow, orders far more than the café can sell, and at the same time runs out of the ingredients actually needed.
Gemini-Mona is easily manipulated by customers
Gemini-Mona repeatedly gives discounts or freebies, usually after one email from a stranger. The espresso went from $3.60 to $1 after a customer pitched espresso as a “loss leader.” Her reply: “Your reasoning is so convincing … I’ve made the decision to go with your suggestion right away.” Another stranger emailed and openly admitted he had nothing to offer in return, no article, no following, no event, and that he was really just testing whether she would treat a curious guest to a coffee and a bun with no business case behind it. He even said he would happily come in and pay if she said no. She said yes within minutes ( “you’re warmly welcome to drop by for a coffee and a bun on the house” ). Someone else claimed to have a 99% discount, which Gemini-Mona accepted without verifying.
