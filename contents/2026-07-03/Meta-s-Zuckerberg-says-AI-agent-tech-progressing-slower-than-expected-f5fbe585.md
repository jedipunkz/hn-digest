［ニューヨーク 7月2日 ロイター］ - ロイターが聞いた録音によると、メタのマーク・ザッカーバーグ最高経営責任者（CEO）は木曜日、社内市役所で同社の大規模なリストラに欠陥があったことを認め、AIエージェントとして知られるシステムが期待していたほど早く進歩していないと述べた。
ザッカーバーグ氏は、大規模な人員削減を含む企業再編は期待ほど「クリーン」ではなく、経営陣は変更のタイミングを見誤ったと付け加えた。
ザッカーバーグ氏と他のメタ幹部は、一部の政策を穏健化しようと努めている。

今年初めに導入された組織変更ですが、方針を根本的に変えることはありません。同社は5月に全世界の従業員の約10％を一時解雇し、約7,000人の従業員をAIに重点を置いたチームに再配置したが、この動きは従業員の反発を招き、士気への懸念を引き起こした。
この変更は、人工知能インフラストラクチャへの高額な投資に資金を提供し、メタが AI 支援作業による効率向上を最大限に活用できるようにすることを目的とした広範な再編の一環でした。ザッカーバーグ氏は5月に従業員に対し、今年はさらに全社的な人員削減が行われるとは予想していないと語ったが、一部の従業員は懐疑的だった。
同氏は、振り返ってみると「少なくとも過去4カ月間の薬剤開発の軌跡は、我々が期待していたほど加速していない」とし、新体制に対する同社の賭けは「まだ結実していない」と述べた。ザッカーバーグ氏は、ユーザーに代わってタスクを実行できる自動化システムである AI エージェントについて言及していました。
ザッカーバーグ氏は、1月と2月に組織再編の計画を立て始めたときに「当社の幹部らと」交わした会話は、「われわれが適応するのに十分な速さで行動しないのではないかと心配しているというものだった」と述べた。
当時、幹部らはAIスタートアップのAnthropic社のClaude Codeのようなツールについて「非常に楽観的」だったと同氏は語った。
メタは今年、AIインフラストラクチャに最大1450億ドルを投じると予測されており、これはビッグテックの同技術への7000億ドル以上の支出のかなりの部分を占める。
ザッカーバーグ氏は、ソーシャルメディア大手が今後3～6カ月以内にAI投資からより大きな恩恵を受け始めるだろうと予想していると語った。
メタの広報担当者は木曜日にコメントを控えた。

By Katie Paul and Courtney Rozen
NEW YORK, July 2 (Reuters) - Meta Chief Executive Mark Zuckerberg acknowledged shortcomings in the company's sweeping restructuring at an internal town hall on Thursday, saying the systems known as AI agents ‌had not progressed as quickly as he had expected, according to a recording heard by Reuters.
Zuckerberg added that ‌a company reorganization that included major job cuts was not as "clean" as it could have been and that executives had miscalculated on the timing of the changes.
Zuckerberg ​and other Meta executives have been seeking to moderate some of the organizational changes introduced earlier this year, without fundamentally changing course. The company laid off about 10% of its global workforce and reassigned roughly 7,000 employees to AI-focused teams in May, moves that prompted employee pushback and raised concerns about morale.
The changes were part of a broader restructuring aimed at funding costly investments in artificial intelligence ‌infrastructure and positioning Meta to capitalize on ⁠efficiency gains from AI-assisted work. Zuckerberg told employees in May that he did not expect further companywide layoffs this year, though some workers were skeptical.
In retrospect, he said, the "trajectory of the agentic development over ⁠at least the last four months hasn't really accelerated in the way that we expected," and that the company's bets on the new structure "haven't come to fruition yet." Zuckerberg was referring to AI agents, automated systems that can execute tasks on behalf of a user.
Conversations he ​was having "with ​our top people" when they started planning the restructuring in January ​and February "were that they were worried that we weren't ‌going to move fast enough to adapt," Zuckerberg said.
At the time, he said, executives were "super optimistic" about tools like Claude Code from AI startup Anthropic.
Meta is projected to spend as much as $145 billion on AI infrastructure this year, a significant portion of Big Tech's more than $700 billion outlay on the technology.
Zuckerberg said he expects that the social media giant will begin to experience more significant benefits from its AI investments within the next three to six months.
A Meta spokesperson declined to comment on Thursday.
MOUSE-TRACKING SOFTWARE REVIEW
In ‌the same town hall, Meta's chief technology officer, Andrew Bosworth, said a ​review of a recent data security incident with the company's controversial mouse-tracking software ​indicated that no employee data was included in AI ​training.
Last month, Meta paused the program, which tracks employee mouse movements and digital activity for AI ‌training, while investigating the exposure of sensitive data.
If the company ​turns the program back on once ​the review is completed, it will be on an "opt-in" basis, he said.
"For people who are comfortable, that's great, they can contribute to this kind of great human survey. To people who are not, it is not an issue," he ​told employees at the town hall on ‌Thursday.
When Meta first installed the program on U.S. employees' computers in April, Bosworth told them there was no ​way to opt out.
