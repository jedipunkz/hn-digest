AI で生成されたコンテンツを開発または使用する企業は、通信事業者が自社の責任を制限しようと競う中、関連する訴訟や規制調査に巻き込まれるか、保険料を鼻から支払ってしまう可能性があります。
業界専門家らによると、一部の保険会社はここ数カ月、ベリスク・アナリティクスの保険サービス部門が昨年利用可能にした標準的な保険契約文言に基づき、生成型人工知能に関連する保険金請求に関して除外措置を採用している。保険会社が商業一般賠償責任保険に付加できるこれらの除外項目は、現時点では人身傷害、物的損害、または損害による損失に焦点を当てています。

保険契約者による生成型 AI の使用によって生じる個人的および広告的損害。
一部の通信事業者はさらに進んでいます。 W. R. バークレー社は昨年、保険契約者の取締役や役員に対する請求を含め、この種では初となる「絶対的」AI 排除措置を発表した。
これらの規定は、生成型 AI の使用を超えるものもあり、保険契約者は、AI 関連の開示や事業運営におけるテクノロジーの監督方法などを含め、幅広い申し立てにさらされることになる。
保険契約者を代表するリード・スミス法律事務所のパートナー、コートニー・ホリガン氏は、「現在、Googleと連携した携帯電話を含むあらゆるものがAIを使用していることを考えると、AIを広範に排除した場合に何が残るのか分からない」と述べた。
裁判所はまだ、生成型 AI の使用に起因する企業の損失に対する保険適用に関する先導的な判決を下していないが、保険契約者とその通信会社にとってのリスクは非常に高い。
仲介業者ギャラガー・リーの報告書によると、米国におけるジェネレーティブAI関連の訴訟は2021年から2025年にかけて978％急増した。

Insurer AI Exclusions Spark Policyholder Alarm on Coverage Gaps
generative artificial intelligence
Companies that develop or use AI-generated content will likely either find themselves on the hook for any related litigation or regulatory probes or paying through the nose for insurance coverage as carriers race to limit their own liability.
Some insurers in recent months have adopted exclusions, based on standard policy language made available last year by Verisk Analytics Inc.'s Insurance Services Office, over claims related to generative artificial intelligence, according to industry professionals. Those exclusions, which insurers can attach to commercial general liability policies, focus for now on losses from bodily injury, property damage, or personal and advertising injury arising from policyholders’ use of generative AI.
Some carriers are going even further. W. R. Berkley Corp. announced a first-of-its-kind “absolute” AI exclusion last year, including for claims against a policyholder’s directors and officers.
Those provisions, some of which go beyond the use of generative AI, are poised to leave policyholders exposed to a wide range of claims, including over their AI-related disclosures and how they oversee the technology in their business operations.
“Given that everything including our phones with Google now uses AI, I don’t know how anything would be left if there is a broad AI exclusion,” said Reed Smith LLP partner Courtney Horrigan, who represents policyholders.
While courts have yet to issue a bellwether ruling on insurance coverage for companies’ losses stemming from their use of generative AI, the stakes are sky-high for policyholders and their carriers.
Generative AI-related lawsuits in the US surged by 978% from 2021 to 2025, according to a report from broker Gallagher Re.
AI litigation more broadly has targeted tech giants such as Anthropic PBC and Microsoft Corp. over their use of copyrighted materials from authors and newspaper publishers to develop AI tools, while other companies stand accused of “AI-washing” — making misleading statements to investors or consumers over AI capabilities.
Many companies “are already relying on systems they cannot fully interrogate, using outputs they do not always challenge, within frameworks that are still evolving,” according to a May report on AI risks by insurance broker Willis Towers Watson Plc.
Some insurers may ultimately adopt narrow exclusions for AI-related claims or offer new product lines to cover them, but policyholders will likely face coverage gaps or expensive premiums in any event, industry professionals said.
“In the immediate future, harm to policyholders is expected, particularly for companies that fail to identify the extent to which AI is embedded in their operations,” Alana McMullin, a policyholder-side partner at Lathrop GPM LLP, said by email. “And narrow exclusions will still create hidden coverage gaps, especially when different policies treat AI differently.”
To appreciate the exponential increase in risks for policyholders and their insurers stemming from AI, consider a company facing property damages at a data center used to operate generative AI and other tools.
“Imagine if there’s a fire or tornado and the data center goes down for a week or a month,” said Ben Lenhart, a co-chair of Covington & Burling LLP’s insurance recovery practice group. “Think of the profits that have been lost.”
Those potential losses could be in the “hundreds of millions, if not a billion” dollar range, Lenhart said.
And that doesn’t include litigation that could arise from downstream vendors, customers, or regulators.
An average 100-megawatt data center, for example, could generate more than $1 billion in annual profits, said Michael Levine, a Hunton Andrews Kurth LLP partner who leads the firm’s property, casualty, and emerging issues insurance practices. If the center went down, it could experience a business interruption loss equaling about 70% of its profit total that would typically be covered by property insurance but may be excluded under the new AI provisions, he said.
“It is far beyond anything that property insurers are currently anticipating as a risk for a commercial facility,” Levine said. “And it’s that magnitude of loss that these centers will experience instantaneously.”
When cyber risks became a priority for the insurance industry several decades ago, some carriers quickly adopted policy exclusions while others offered new product lines.
“It’s all about risk,” said Elliot Chen, a Ervin Cohen & Jessup LLP partner and policyholder-side attorney who specializes in insurance coverage litigation. “When one insurer excludes a risk, someone tends to step in and pick it up.”
Responding to AI risks, some carriers have chosen to add language to existing lines to clarify what is covered, rather than actively excluding generative AI and related claims from their policies, industry professionals said.
“You’re going to see a lot of innovation around ensuring policies evolve to cover new AI exposures,” said Paul Bantick, Beazley Group’s chief underwriting officer.
Many insurers eventually developed ways to measure the risks of cyberattacks against their policyholders. When it comes to AI, however, carriers are grappling with underwriting uncertainty around novel uses of the technology and the potential for aggregated losses when multiple policyholders use the same tools, among other challenges.
“The issue is that AI just remains a new and fast-developing risk, so it’s not unusual for insurers to take a cautious approach while the exposure continues to mature,” said Jeff Kulikowski, Westfield Specialty’s executive vice president and head of cyber and professional liability.
While the industry figures out how to exclude or measure risks arising from AI-related claims against policyholders, the threat of litigation will only continue to grow. In many cases, companies will likely have to pay out of their own pockets for suits over their executives’ statements regarding AI use, losses tied to AI-generated content, and a wide array of other claims.
“Many companies are facing increasing commercial pressure to adopt AI to remain competitive without adequately considering the extent this adoption changes their risk profile,” McMullin said.
