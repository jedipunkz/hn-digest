The 2025–2026 Evolution of Generative Spatial AI
From text-to-mesh and cinematic video to interactive world models, camera-controllable generation, local
production pipelines, and AI-native CAD — a technical retrospective on the rapid maturation of spatial
generative systems.
In roughly thirteen months, generative AI for spatial content has moved from impressive but limited
asset creation tools to systems capable of producing production-oriented 3D meshes, controllable
multi-view imagery, interactive navigable worlds, and even parametric CAD from images or text. What
began with better meshes and short video clips has evolved into chained creator workflows, local-first
tools, and early programmable world models.
This post traces that trajectory through key public announcements and demonstrations shared on X between
May 2025 and June 2026. These posts — from researchers, builders, and curators — provide a real-time map
of technical progress, adoption patterns, and remaining limitations.
May–June 2025: Establishing High-Quality 3D Assets and Video Foundations
The period began with releases focused on production-usable 3D geometry and textures, alongside strong
advances in video generation that could be combined with 3D pipelines.
Meta's AssetGen 2.0
introduced dedicated models for generating meshes and textures from text or images, with explicit
discussion around game engine compatibility (Blender/Unreal pipelines). This represented an important
step beyond earlier research prototypes toward assets that could be directly imported and used.
Tencent's PrimitiveAnything
(shared via Gradio on Hugging Face) demonstrated autoregressive decomposition of complex objects into
editable geometric primitives. This approach offered better editability than pure mesh or implicit
representations for certain design and game workflows.
Video generation saw significant quality jumps.
Google Veo 2
became freely accessible in Google AI Studio (no Advanced subscription required), delivering strong
physics simulation and cinematic quality in short clips. Early experiments combined
FLUX
with Hunyuan 3D models to create pseudo-3D renders, highlighting both the promise of hybrid 2D-to-3D
pipelines and the remaining gaps in true mesh consistency and topology.
By June,
Hunyuan-3D-2.1
delivered notable improvements in texture quality with open weights available on Hugging Face. Research
such as
FreeTimeGS
advanced 4D Gaussian Splatting techniques for dynamic scenes with complex motion, pointing toward future
dynamic world reconstruction capabilities.
These releases established that high-quality 3D asset generation and supporting video tools were
becoming accessible building blocks rather than purely research artifacts.

August 2025 marked a fundamental shift with
Google DeepMind's Genie 3

text-to-interactive-3D-worlds

Early–Mid 2026: Camera Control, Vibe Coding at Scale, Local Tools, Playable Worlds, and AI-Native CAD
The first half of 2026 brought further democratization through better controllability, local execution,
practical creator workflows, and early steps into fully playable generated content and parametric
design.
Camera control and multi-view consistency
advanced with custom Gradio components for camera-control LoRAs and accessible tools that let users
upload an image and orbit a virtual camera to generate consistent new angles. These proved immediately
useful for animation and shot variety.
"Vibe coding" workflows
became more sophisticated and widely demonstrated. Builders showed complete pipelines for game assets
and UIs: image/character design with tools like Nano Banana or Midjourney → 3D conversion via Hunyuan3D
→ UI logic and interaction via Gemini Pro. Detailed threads covered rigging (Mixamo), material handling,
iteration strategies, and realistic time estimates (often a few hours for impressive functional
results). These examples illustrated how specialized models could be chained for rapid prototyping by
individuals without traditional 3D or coding expertise.
Public access to world models expanded with the rollout of
Genie 3
to Google AI Ultra subscribers, generating significant discussion around interactive environment
generation.
A notable development was
Moonlake
(powered by the Reverie engine), which demonstrated text prompts generating fully playable 3D
games/worlds with NPCs, physics, multiplayer support, and persistent state. Unlike pre-rendered video,
these were interactive systems where changes (destruction, weather, restyling entire aesthetics while
preserving mechanics) persisted. The project highlighted strong backing and positioned itself as a

Microsoft released

noted VRAM requirements and that it represented continued progress in fast, usable inference.
End-to-end asset creation pipelines were heavily discussed, with some claims of dramatically reduced
timelines for game-ready assets. These sparked both excitement and realistic pushback regarding topology
quality, the need for cleanup, authorship, and true production readiness.

AI-native CAD
began emerging. Tools demonstrated converting images or generated concepts into editable parametric CAD
models (STEP format), with examples including detailed mechanical objects like watches. Projects such as
ForgeCAD and MIT's GenCAD pointed toward future workflows where design intent could be expressed in
natural language or images and directly yield editable engineering models.

Technical Insights and Remaining Challenges
Several clear technical trends emerge across this period:
Controllability improved
through LoRAs, custom camera interfaces, and promptable systems, moving beyond one-shot generation.
Topology and production readiness
advanced (optimized meshes in Hunyuan 3D 3.0, better handling of complex geometry in TRELLIS.2), but
cleanup, UVs, and rigging often remain necessary for high-end use.
Local and open-source inference
became viable for individuals (Modly, TRELLIS.2), reducing cost and privacy barriers.
From assets to systems
: The field progressed from static meshes to interactive worlds with state (Genie 3, Moonlake/Reverie)
and parametric outputs (ForgeCAD, GenCAD).
Chained workflows
("vibe coding") proved effective for rapid prototyping, though they still benefit from human iteration
and artistic direction.
Persistent challenges include long-term coherence in generated worlds, advanced rigging/animation at
scale, seamless engine integration, material/physics authoring, and the gap between impressive demos and
robust production assets. Skepticism in community replies often correctly highlights these realities.
Conclusion: The Pieces Are Arriving Faster Than Expected
The X posts from May 2025 through mid-2026 document the construction of generative spatial computing in
public. We now have accessible high-quality asset generation, camera-controllable imagery, local fast 3D
tools, early text-to-playable interactive systems, and initial AI-native CAD capabilities.
Gaps remain, but the velocity and direction are unmistakable. The combination of open research,
practical creator experimentation, and major lab releases (Google, Microsoft, Tencent, Meta, and others)
has compressed what once took years into months.
The "holodeck" is not arriving as a single product. It is being assembled, piece by piece, through these
incremental but compounding advances — and the public sharing of models, demos, and workflows on
platforms like X has been one of the most effective ways to track and accelerate that progress.
The question for builders in mid-2026 is no longer
whether
these capabilities will exist, but
how quickly
we can integrate them into real products, games, tools, and creative processes — and what new forms of
spatial expression they will enable.
