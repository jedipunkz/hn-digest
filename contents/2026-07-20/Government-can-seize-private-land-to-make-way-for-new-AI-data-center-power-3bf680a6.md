この法律は、土地が公共利用であり、所有者が正当な補償を受けている限り、所有者の同意に関係なく、私有地を収用する権限を政府に与えている。政府は電力会社などの「民間団体または通信事業者」に権限を委任することもできる。電力会社が買収を実行するために制定できるのは、この法的権限です。
報告書によると、法律は自動的に無謬の権限を付与するものではない。企業はインフラが公共利用であることを証明する必要があります。いくつかの州は、独自の憲法に従って著名なドメイン法を解釈する権利を留保しています。

見出しは事実として間違っています。民間団体には著名な権限はなく、政府だけが権限を持っています。配電会社は「新しいAIデータセンターの送電線を建設するために私有地を占拠する」ことはできないからだ。彼らはまずその土地を購入するか、または通行権について所有者と合意に達することを試みなければなりません。それができない場合は、政府に対し、所有者から土地を市場価格で購入するよう要求しなければならないが、政府はこれを「公共の利益」の目的で行う。それには、法的申し立て、裁判官、裁判所、判決を含む法的枠組み全体が存在します。
地域社会の怒りが十分にあれば、選挙で選ばれた政治家が介入して、それを中止させるだろう。それ以外の場合、特に公共の利益のためではない場合には、採取を阻止する法的差し止め命令も使用される可能性があります。

A report by The Conversation has claimed that the government can seize private land to make way for new transmission lines needed to meet the surging electricity demand of data centers. According to the July 16 report by Aaron Walayat, Assistant Professor of Law at the University of Dayton, power companies can use eminent domain — the legal authority that grants the government the power to take private property and convert it to public use in exchange for compensation — to implement the takeovers.

The AI boom has led to a surge in data centers in the U.S., with thousands already operational and several more planned or under construction. While the massive superclusters are necessary for the technological revolution AI has brought about, there's growing opposition to their construction over several concerns. Among them are land use, noise pollution, water usage, and the impact of immense electricity consumption , issues that have reportedly made 70% of Americans opposed to building data centers nearby .
In many instances, data centers draw the required electricity from the grid. As the industry enters the gigawatt era, utility companies are under pressure to increase supply to meet surging demand. This requires building new power infrastructure, such as transmission lines, which often have to cross private land. When this happens, the power companies try to buy the land. Should the owner refuse, the government can force a sale through the eminent domain law.
The law grants the government the power to take private land, regardless of the owner's consent, provided that the land is for public use and the owner receives just compensation. The government can also delegate the power to “private entities or common carriers,” such as utility companies. It is this legal authority that power companies can enact to implement the takeovers.
According to the report, the law does not automatically grant infallible authority. The company must prove that the infrastructure will be for public use. Several states also reserve the right to interpret eminent domain laws according to their own constitutions.

The report highlights another layer of legalities amid ongoing data center tensions. Opponents have successfully blocked 75 planned data center projects in the first quarter of 2026 , including the 2,100-acre Digital Gateway project, eventually canceled over a newspaper-notice technicality . On the other hand, several other projects have gone ahead, often with community support. Meta recently announced plans to expand its Hyperion AI supercluster from 2 GW to 5 GW .

Headline is factually incorrect. Private entities do not have the power of eminent domain, only governments do. there for power distribution companies can not "seize private land to make way for new AI data center transmission lines". They must first attempt to either purchase that land or come to an agreement with the owners over right of way. Failing that, they must then request the government to purchase the land from the owners at market value, which the government does for the purpose of the "common good". There is an entire legal framework around that which includes legal motions, Judges, courts and rulings.
If there is sufficient community outrage, the elected politicians will intervene and get it canceled. Otherwise legal injunctions preventing the takings can also be used, especially if it's not for the common good.
TH needs to be careful about putting too much trust into opinion pieces written by university professors.
