先月、このプログラムは Quantum Genesis イニシアチブを発表し、スーパーコンピューティングと AI とともに、将来の HPC における重要な新機能の原動力となると期待されるものの第 3 段階を導入しました。この発表は、DoEが「科学的に関連性のある」フォールトトレラント量子システムに関する情報提供の要請を受け、2028年までに研究開発プログラム向けに世界初のそのようなコンピューターを開発・配備するという野心的な計画を策定したことを受けて行われた。
業界の多くの人々は、実用的なフォールトトレラントな量子システムがおそらくこの 10 年後半か来年初めに登場するだろうと予想しています。そうは言っても、業界には政府資金の流入が見込まれており、連邦政府機関は明らかに開発努力とスケジュールが加速すると期待しているようだ。
今週、業界はジェネシス・ミッションにどのような資金が投じられているのか、またその目的は何なのかをよく調べた。

政府はそれに期待しています。ワシントン DC で開催された第 1 回ジェネシス・ミッション・サミットで、DoE はこのイニシアチブによって資金提供される最初の 278 プロジェクトを発表しました。大統領補佐官でホワイトハウス科学技術政策局長のマイケル・クラツィオス氏は、ジェネシス・ミッションを拡大するために連邦政府から50億ドル以上の約束がなされていると述べた。
さらに、クラツィオスは、ジェネシス・ミッションの国家科学技術課題を発表しました。これは、ヘルスケアやエネルギーから製造、インフラストラクチャー、手頃な価格に至るまで、幅広い優先事項に触れており、参加者はイニシアチブの枠組みを利用して取り組むことができます。たとえば、慢性疾患の根本原因の解明に取り組んでいる人々は、保健福祉省、環境保護庁、国立科学財団からのデータを利用できるようになり、一方で DoE は AI ベースのデジタル ツインを利用して国の電力網を改善することができます。

DoE Fires The $5 Billion Starter Gun For Its AI-Targeted Genesis Mission
In the eight months since its creation, the leaders of the US government’s Genesis Mission, which aims to use emerging AI capabilities in concert with supercomputers and, more recently, quantum computing , to advance science, has been putting various pieces in place to meet its mandate.
The Department of Energy, which President Trump tasked to lead the initiative when he signed the initial executive order in November 2025, in May created the Genesis Mission Consortium to expand the program’s private-public partnership nature. The Consortium now includes more than 340 members, including 157 private companies, 142 universities, and DoE and National Nuclear Security Administration’s 16 national laboratories.
Last month, the program unveiled the Quantum Genesis initiative , bringing in the third leg – along with supercomputing and AI – of what is expected to be drivers of significant new capabilities in HPC of the future. The announcement followed a request for information put out by the DoE about “scientifically relevant” fault-tolerant quantum systems and put in place an ambition plan to develop and deploy the world’s first such computer for R&D programs by 2028.
The expectation of many in the industry is that practical, fault-tolerant quantum systems are likely coming later this decade or early in the next. That said, the industry now will see an influx of government money coming into the mix, which federal agencies apparently expect will accelerate development efforts and the timeline.
The industry this week got a good look at what kind of money is being pushed to Genesis Mission and what the government expects from it. At the first Genesis Mission Summit in Washington, DC, the DoE announced the first 278 projects that will be funded by the initiative. Michael Kratsios, assistant to the president and director of the White House Office of Science and Technology Policy, said more than $5 billion in federal commitments are being made to expand the Genesis Mission.
In addition, Kratsios announced the Genesis Mission’s National Science and Technology Challenges , which touch on a range of priorities from healthcare and energy to manufacturing, infrastructure, and affordability, and which participants can use the initiatives framework to address. For example , those working on finding the root causes of chronic disease will be able to use data from the Health and Human Services department, the Environmental Protection Agency, and the National Science Foundation, while the DoE will use AI-based digital twins to improve the country’s power grid.
Other challenges including detecting biological threats early and attributing them to sources, accelerating the design of weapons systems, and creating autonomous, AI-driven laboratories.
Along with the announcements rolling out from the Genesis Mission, Microsoft – a member of the project’s partner consortium – said it is investing $60 million to support the efforts. According to Chris Barry, Microsoft’s president of US public sectors industry group, $40 million will be available in Azure cloud compute and AI credits that will go to researchers over the next three years to help pay large-scale AI and scientific workloads. The other $20 million will go towards engineering, architecture, deployment, and adoption support to expand cloud and AI capacity. This is obviously a mere drop in the bucket, of course. But Microsoft no doubt wants to be in the front line when and if Genesis projects decide to rent rather than build AI supercomputers.
In addition, Microsoft is launching Scientific Partnership Advancing Research and Knowledge (SPARK) to coordinate the vendor’s work with the Genesis Mission, offering access to the Microsoft Discovery platform that includes AI models, simulations, data, and experimental workflows, and promoting the work it’s doing in quantum research, including with its Majorana quantum chip (below).
HPE said it has been selected for six of the projects , and that it is building Lux (below), a dedicated AI system that will be housed at the Oak Ridge National Laboratory and will be the first such supercomputer for science to support the Genesis Mission.
The Genesis Mission projects a massive, all-of-government, public-private effort with a lot of money being pushed to fund big projects, an initiative that some members are equating with the World War II-era Manhattan Project to build the first nuclear bomb and NASA’s Apollo moon-landing program.
Earlier this month, Kratsios sent a report to the White House titled “ Science: A New Golden Age ,” outlining the goals of the Genesis Mission, including ensuring the United States remains the leading power in emerging and critical technology at a time of growing competition with the likes of China, and how to overcome obstacles to success.
The report also talks about the need to revitalize the country’s science and technology sectors and ensure that the work they do improves the lives of Americans.
“We can only fully harness AI and its associated productivity uplift by boldly reforming our scientific institutions, building national-scale infrastructure, and ensuring rigorous verification of the knowledge base from which AI will learn,” Kratsios wrote.
The Genesis Mission offers lofty goals, but comes against the backdrop of ongoing realities that include the continuous budget fights on Capitol Hill that, most recently, is seeing the House of Representatives having to again consider a continuing resolution to ensure the government stays open after the fiscal year ends in September if a new budget can’t be agreed upon by then.
The Genesis Mission leaders also will have to sell the efforts to the public and some organizations that may support the aims of the project but not necessarily the means. For example, after the DoE earlier this year unveiled 26 energy-related challenges it wanted to address, the Institute for Progress last week urged the federal agency to streamline its goals and messaging. The Genesis Mission can reach its intentions of driving AI-fueled scientific discoveries via federal resources, industry, academia, and the country’s national labs, but it will need clear goals, according to Dan Turner-Evans, Senior Metascience Fellow at the IFP.
“An overly broad Genesis Mission that fails to deliver will lower overall long-term support for AI at DOE, especially at a time when AI polls poorly with the public ,” Turners-Evans wrote. “A handful of successful demonstrations will make a better case for sustained support, allowing DOE to expand AI across more priority areas and ultimately bring more resources to the national labs, all while demonstrating tangible wins to Congress and to Americans.”
That said, the Genesis Mission comes at an important time for the United States, according to the Center for Strategic and International Studies (CSIS). In a report weeks after Trump’s executive order creating the project, CSIS’ Navin Girishankar, president of its Economic Security and Technology Department, and Chris Borges, Fellow and the department’s senior program manager, wrote the “high level of ambition” is important for two reasons .
The first is China’s progress in challenging the United States in advanced research facilities, talent, and AI-based discovery platforms, and in such key areas as battery chemistry, nuclear energy, and quantum computing.
“China is not going to slow down its scientific efforts: early indications suggest that China’s upcoming five-year plan will continue to emphasize scientific advancement, and Beijing continues to invest heavily in STEM education, large-scale research facilities, and AI-driven research platforms,” Girishankar and Borges wrote.
They also noted the United States’ “stalled” research productivity, pointing to trend of the expenditures and number of researchers – researching inputs – rising faster than outputs, like breakthroughs and productivity.
Why this is happening in unclear, they wrote, noting that one theory holds that new discoveries are more difficult to come by as fields advance, that essentially the easier questions have been answered. Others point to artificial boundaries going up between scientific disciplines driven by academic organizations. The result is scientists are funneled into increasingly narrow problems, which hobbles broader advances.
“Regardless of the cause, the administration has consistently embraced the view that scientific productivity has stalled and that AI can reverse the trend,” they wrote, adding that “the launch of Genesis is well-timed. Scientists are already adopting new scientific discovery tools at scale, with demand expected to grow exponentially. A massive GenAI infrastructure build-out is underway that can provide the much-needed compute power American scientists will need.”
